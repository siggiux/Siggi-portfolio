01 — Overview

Designing an operator portal that enables tour operators to manage last-minute discounts at scale.

Hot Tickets, a company I co-founded, is a marketplace for bookable tourism experiences in Iceland. It connects travellers with last-minute discounted tours from local tour operators.

To sell these tours, we relied on availability data from Bókun, the industry standard platform where tour operators manage pricing and inventory. Depending on Bókun's limited discount tools became a bottleneck that slowed our growth.

To scale the business, I designed a dedicated portal where discounts could be managed. This case study walks through the process behind designing that operator portal.

03 — Product brief

Replacing a fragile setup to enable Hot Tickets to scale.

As we aimed to grow the company, relying on Bókun's built-in discount tooling became a limiting factor. Discounts, a core part of our business model, were configured inside a system we did not control, using workflows that were confusing for operators and often required hands-on, in-person support.

This setup worked initially but did not scale. Expanding into new markets made it clear that Hot Tickets needed a dedicated operator-facing web application.

Goals

  • Increase trust in the operator onboarding process
  • Enable simple, self-service discount setup for non-technical operators
  • Give operators clear visibility into discount impact and performance

Constraints

  • Bókun remained the system of record for inventory and bookings
  • Integration had to work within Bókun's API limitations
  • Limited development resources required pragmatic scope decisions

04 — Research

Using research to understand the domain and identify risk.

The purpose of the research phase was to ensure the operator portal would cover all essential needs, while also validating where deeper, insight-driven design was required.

To ensure that research addressed the full scope of the product I started by clustering the problem space into key topic areas. This ensured that research addressed the full scope of the product.

I then mapped out what was already known based on operating Hot Tickets and onboarding operators, and contrasted this with what still needed to be learned.

I then turned my "need to learn" column into research questions. These were the high-level questions my research needed to answer.

Research was conducted through three qualitative interviews with tour operators. I intentionally selected operators with different business profiles to better represent the diversity of Hot Tickets's partner base.

These interviews were complemented by ongoing observational research through real onboarding sessions, support conversations, and email exchanges with operators. In parallel, desk research and hands-on exploration of Bókun were used to understand existing workflows, system behavior, and technical constraints.

05 — System mapping

Mapping the portal as a complete system before designing any screens.

With research in place, the next step was to map the operator portal as a complete system. The goal at this stage was to understand everything operators needed to do for the product to work end to end, before focusing on individual solutions.

This mapping exercise made it clear which parts of the product were largely conventional and could rely on established patterns, and which areas carried higher uncertainty. The discount creation flow emerged as the most complex and business-critical part of the system.
